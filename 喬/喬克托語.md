'''喬克托語'''(英語：Choctaw；喬克托語：Chahta')是一種[[穆斯科吉語言|穆斯科吉語族]](Muskogean languages)的語言，傳統上通行於美國東南部的原住民族群之一[[喬克托人|喬克托人]]之間。儘管有人將[[契卡索語|契卡索語]](Chickasaw)列為喬克托語的其中一種方言，但更多的資料指出，應將契卡索和喬克托語給視為彼此相似但不同的語言。<ref>Munro 1984</ref>

==正寫法==
喬克托語的書寫系統基於英語字母，儘管喬克托語有多種不同的書寫系統，但目最常見的系統為拜印頓系統(Byington (Original)、拜印頓─蘇萬敦系統(Byington/Swanton (Linguistic))和密西西比州喬克托族的現代系統(Modern (Mississippi Choctaw))。

====循韻長音化====
* '''循韻長音化'''Rhythmic lengthening 在喬克托語中是一個將第偶數個以CV形式出現的音節的母音拉長的過程。然而這過程有個限制，那就是單詞結尾的母音不能經由此過程拉長；此外，I類或III類的動詞前綴所擁有的母音亦不能套用此過程，故若某個處於第偶數個音節位置的音節是屬某個I類或III類的動詞前綴所有的話，該詞綴所擁有的母音亦不可經此程序拉長，而對III類名詞前綴亦有相同的限制。<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=21–26}}</ref>如下所示：
: CV-CV-CVC→CV-CV<big>''':'''</big>-CVC
:salahatok→sala:hatok

====最短可能單詞====
* 喬克托語中可能出現的最短單詞必須有兩個短母音或一個長母音。<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=18–20}}</ref>如下所示：
: a:t

* /A-/ 插入：在喬克托語中，有些動詞其詞根形式只包含一個短母音且沒有其他詞綴附著於這些動詞的詞根上，而這使得這些動詞詞根在喬克托語中變成不可能的存在，因為喬克托語中可能出現的最短單詞必須有兩個短母音或一個長母音。為了解決這情況，在實際言談中，一個A-前綴會附於這些詞根的開頭。<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=18–21}}</ref>如下所示：
: *bih → a-bih

====與/-li/這個後綴相關的音變過程====

First when the verbal suffix /-li/ is preceded by any of the following consonants /f/ /ɫ/ /h/ /m/ /n/ or /w/, the /l/ assimilates to the corresponding consonant that precedes it.<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=26–27}}</ref> Second, when the verbal suffix /-li/ is preceded by the consonant /b/, the /l/ is realized as /b/.<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=26–27}}</ref> Third, when the verbal suffix /-li/ is preceded by the consonant /p/, the /p/ is realized as /b/.<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=26–27}}</ref> Lastly, when the verbal suffix /-li/ is preceded by the consonant /t/, the /t/ is realized as /l/.<ref name="Broadwell 2006">{{cite book|last=Broadwell|first=George Aaron|title=A Choctaw Reference Grammar|year=2006|publisher=University of Nebraska Press|location=Lincoln, NE|isbn=0-8032-1315-8|pages=26–27}}</ref>

===動詞形態===
Choctaw verbs display a wide range of inflectional and derivational morphology.  In Choctaw, the category of verb may also include words that would be categorized as adjectives or quantifiers in English.  Verbs may be preceded by up to three prefixes and followed by as many as five suffixes.  In addition, verb roots may contain infixes that convey aspectual information.

====動詞前綴====
The verbal prefixes convey information about the arguments of the verb—how many there are and their person and number features. The prefixes can be divided into three sorts: [[Agreement_(linguistics)|agreement]] markers, applicative markers, and [[Anaphora_(linguistics)|anaphors]] (reflexives and reciprocals). These prefixes occur in the following order:
: Agreement-Anaphor-Applicative-Verb Stem

I類、II類和III類是對於三種人稱變化模式的中性稱呼。有些作者(Ulrich 1986, Davies, 1986)以「施事─受事─所與」（actor–patient–dative）或「主語─賓語─所與」（nominative–accusative–dative）之類的名稱稱之。

第一人稱I類一致的標記為/-li/，它是一致標記中唯一的後綴。它將在此段中與其他的一致標記一同討論。

I類、II類和III類一致標記的使用法則根據論元的種類而定。及物動態動詞（Transitive active verbs）是其中最具可預測性的模式。在通常的及物動態動詞中，主詞使用I類標記、直接受詞使用II類標記、間接受詞使用III類標記。

如上所見，處於第三人稱的論元沒有一致標記。讀者可見下範例之說明：
* ''Habli-li-tok''（意即「我踢了他(們)」）
* ''Ish-habli-tok''（意即「你踢了他(們)」）
* ''Habli-tok''（意即「他(們)踢了他(們)」）
* ''Ii-habli-tok''（意即「我們踢了他(們)」）
* ''Hash-habli-tok''（意即「你們踢了他(們)」）

* ''Sa-habli-tok''（意即「他(們)踢了我」）
* ''Chi-habli-tok''（意即「他(們)踢了你」）
* ''Habli-tok''（意即「他(們)踢了他(們)」）
* ''Pi-habli-tok''（意即「他(們)踢了我們」）
* ''Hachi-habli-tok''（意即「他(們)踢了你們」）

* ''Am-anoli-tok''（意即「他(們)告訴了我」）
* ''Chim-anoli-tok''（意即「他(們)告訴了你」）
* ''Im-anoli-tok''（意即「他(們)告訴了他(們)」）
* ''Pim-anoli-tok''（意即「他(們)告訴了我們」）
* ''Hachim-anoli-tok''（意即「他(們)告訴了你們」）

=====否定=====
N類一致標記用於否定中。<ref>Broadwell (2006:148-152)</ref> Negation is multiply marked, requiring that an agreement marker from the N set replace the ordinary I agreement, the verb appear in the lengthened grade (see discussion below), and that the suffix /-o(k)-/ follow the verb, with deletion of the preceding final vowel. The optional suffix /-kii/may be added after /-o(k)-/. Consider the following example:
* ''Akíiyokiittook.''
* ''Ak-íiya-o-kii-ttook''
* ''1sN-go<lgr>-neg-neg-dpast''
*中譯：我沒去

可將此句和其相對應的肯定句進行比較：
* ''Iyalittook''
* ''Iya-li-ttook''.
* ''go-1sI-dpast''
*中譯：我去了

To make this example negative,  the 1sI suffix /-li/ is replaced by the 1sN prefix /ak-/; the verb root iya is lengthened and accented to yield íiya; the suffix /-o/ is added, the final vowel of iiya is deleted; and the suffix /-kii/ is added.

=====呼應前綴=====
Reflexives are indicated with the /ili-/ prefix, and reciprocals with /itti-/:<ref>Broadwell (2006:98-99)</ref>
* ''Ilipísalitok''.
* ''li-p<u>í</u>sa-li-tok''.
* ''refl-see<ngr>-1sI-pt''
* 'I saw myself'.

====Verb suffixes====
	While the verbal prefixes indicate relations between the verb and its arguments, the suffixes cover a wider semantic range, including information about valence, modality, tense and evidentiality.

The following examples show modal and tense suffixes like /-aachi<u>i</u>/ 'irrealis'(approximately equal to future), /-tok/ 'past tense', /-h/ 'default tenses':<ref>Broadwell (2006:169-183)</ref>

	Baliilih.	<br />
	Baliili-h.<br />
	run-tns<br />
	`She runs.'

	Baliilaach<u>i</u>h.	<br />
	Baliili-aach<u>i</u>-h.	<br />
	run-irr-tns<br />
	`She will run.'

There are also suffixes that show evidentiality, or the source of evidence for a statement, as in the following pair:<ref>Broadwell (2006:184-190)</ref>

	Nipi’ awashlihli.<br />
	Nipi’ awashli-hli <br />
	meat fry-first:hand	<br />
	`She fried the meat.' (I saw/heard/smelled her do it.)

	Nipi’ awashlitok<u>a</u>sha.<br />
	Nipi’ awashli-tok-<u>a</u>sha<br />
	meat  fry-pt-guess

	`She fried the meat.' (I guess)

There are also suffixes of illocutionary force which may indicate that the sentence is a question, an exclamation, or a command:<ref>Broadwell (2006:191-193)</ref>

	Awashlitok<u>o<u>?<br />
	Awashli-tok-<u>o</u><br />
	fry-pt-q<br />
	`Did she fry it?'

	Chahta’  siahokii!	<br />
	Chahta’  si-a-h-okii<br />
	Choctaw 1sII-be-tns-excl<br />
	`I'm Choctaw!' or `I certainly am a Choctaw!'

====Verbal infixes====
Choctaw verb stems have various infixes that indicate their aspect.<ref>Broadwell (2006:161-168)</ref>  These stem variants are traditionally referred to as `grades'.  The table below shows the grades of Choctaw, along with their main usage.

{| class="wikitable"
|-
! Name of Grade
! How it is formed
! When it is used
|-
| n-grade
| infix n in the next to last (penultimate) syllable; put accent on this syllable
| to show that the action is durative (lasts some definite length of time)
|-
| l-grade
| put accent on next to last (penultimate) syllable; lengthen the vowel if the syllable is open
| before a few common suffixes, such as the negative /-o(k)/ and the switch-reference markers /-cha/ and /-na/
|-
| hn-grade
| insert a new syllable /-h<u>V</u>/ after the (original) next to last (penultimate) syllable.  <u>V</u> is a nasalized copy of the vowel that precedes it.
| to show that the action of the verb repeats
|-
| y-grade
| insert -Vyy- before the next to last (penultimate) syllable
| to show delayed inception
|-
| g-grade
| formed by lengthening the penultimate vowel of the stem, accenting the antepenultimate vowel, and geminating the consonant that follows the antepenult.
| to show delayed inception
|-
| h-grade
| insert -h- after the penultimate vowel of the stem.
| to show sudden action
|}

Some examples that show the grades follow:

In this example the l-grade appears because of the suffixes /-na/ 'different subject' and /-o(k)/ 'negative':

''... lowat  táahana      falaamat akíiyokiittook.''<br />
	    lowa-t táaha-na     falaama-t ak-íiya-o-kii-ttook<br />
	    burn-ss complete<lgr>-ds return-ss 1sN-go<lgr>-neg-neg-dpast

		`... (the school) burned down and I didn't go back.'

The g-grade and y-grade typically get translated into English as "finally VERB-ed":

Taloowah.<br/>
	Taloowa-h <br/>
	sing-tns <br/>
	`He sang.'

	Tálloowah.<br/>
	Tálloowa-h <br/>
	sing<ggr>-tns <br/>
	`He finally sang.'

The hn-grade is usually translated as 'kept on VERBing':

	Oh<u>ó</u>bana   nittak pókkooli’ oshtattook.<br />
	Oh<u>ó</u>ba-na  nittak pókkooli’ oshta-ttook<br />
	rain<hngr>-ds day  ten      four-dpast

	`It kept on raining for forty days.'

The h-grade is usually translated "just VERB-ed" or "VERB-ed for a short time":

{{Lang|cho|Nóhsih}}.<br/>
	{{Lang|cho|Nóhsi-h}}<br/>
	sleep<hgr>-tns<br/>
	`He took a quick nap.

===Nominal morphology===
====Noun prefixes====
Nouns have prefixes that show agreement with a possessor.<ref>Broadwell (2006:52-63)</ref> Agreement markers from class II are used on a lexically specified closed class of nouns, which includes many (but not all) of the kinship terms and body parts.  This is the class that is generally labeled [[Inalienable_noun|inalienable]].

	sanoshkobo’		`my head'	<br />
		sa-noshkobo’ <br />
		1sII-head

		chinoshkobo’		`your head' <br />
		chi-noshkobo’	<br />
		2sII-head

		noshkobo’			`his/her/its/their head' <br />
		noshkobo’ <br />
		head

sashki’				`my mother' <br />
		sa-ishki’ <br />
		1sII-mother

		chishki’			`your mother' <br />
		chi-ishki’		<br />
		2sII-mother

Nouns that are not lexically specified for II agreement use the III agreement markers:

	<u>a</u>ki’				`my father'	<br />
		<u>a</u>-ki’ <br />
		1sIII-father

amofi’			`my dog' <br />
		am-ofi’			<br />
		1sIII-dog

Although systems of this type are generally described with the terms alienable and inalienable, this terminology is not particularly appropriate for Choctaw, since alienability implies a semantic distinction between types of nouns.  The morphological distinction between nouns taking II agreement and III agreement in Choctaw only partly coincides with the semantic notion of alienability.

====Noun suffixes====
Choctaw nouns can be followed by various determiner and case-marking suffixes, as in the following examples, where we see
determiners such as /-ma/ `that', /-pa/ `this', and /-akoo/ `contrast' and case-markers /-(y)at/ 'nominative' and /-(y)<u>a</u>/ 'accusative':<ref>Broadwell (2006:64-92)</ref>

alla’  naknimat		<br />
		alla’  nakni-m-at	<br />
		child male-that-nom<br />
		`that boy (nominative)'

Hoshiit  	itti chaahamak<u>o</u>       <u>o</u>biniilih.<br />
		Hoshi’-at itti’ chaaha-m-ak<u>o</u>     <u>o</u>-biniili-h <br />
		bird-nom  	tree tall-that-cntr:acc  superessive-sit-tns <br />
		`The bird is sitting on that tall tree.' (Not on the short one.)

The last example shows that nasalizing the last vowel of the preceding N is a common way to show the accusative case.

===Word order and case marking===
	The simplest sentences in Choctaw consist of a verb and a tense marker, as in the following examples:<ref>Broadwell (2006:32)</ref>

''<u>O</u>batok.''	<br />
<u>O</u>ba-tok<br />
rain-pt<br />
'It rained.'

''Niyah.''	<br />
Niya-h<br />
fat-tns	<br />
'She/he/it is fat.'<br />
'They are fat.'

''P<u>í</u>satok.''	<br />
P<u>í</u>sa-tok<br />
see<ngr>-pt	<br />
'She/he/it/they saw her/him/it/them.'

	As these examples show, there are no obligatory noun phrases in a Choctaw sentence, nor is there any verbal agreement that indicates a third person subject or object. There is no indication of grammatical gender, and for third person arguments there is no indication of number. (There are, however, some verbs with suppletive forms that indicate the number of a subject or object, e.g. iyah `to go (sg.)', ittiyaachih `to go (du.)', and ilhkolih `to go (pl)'.)

When there is an overt subject, it is obligatorily marked with the nominative case /-at/.  Subjects precede the verb

''Hoshiyat  apatok.'' <br>
		Hoshi'-at apa-tok <br>
		bird-nom   eat-pt <br>
		`The birds ate them.'

When there is an overt object, it is optionally marked with the accusative case /-<u>a</u>/:<br />
''Hoshiyat sh<u>o</u>shi(-y<u>a</u>) apatok.''<br />
Hoshi'-at sh<u>o</u>shi'(-<u>a</u>) apa-tok.<br />
bird-nom  bug-(acc)                 eat-pt<br />
'The birds ate the bugs.'

The Choctaw sentence is normally verb-final, and so the head of the sentence is last.
<br />
Some other phrases in Choctaw also have their head at the end.  Possessors precede the possessed noun in the Noun Phrase:

	''ofi' hohchifo'''	<br />
		dog name  <br />
		'the dog's name'

Choctaw has postpositional phrases with the postposition after its object:

''tamaaha' bil<u>i</u>ka''	<br />
		town    near<br />
	`near a town'
